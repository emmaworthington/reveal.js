What is the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation?
Global thermohaline circulation

(thermo = temperature; haline = salinity)

Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation

(AMOC)

Very large amounts of heat moved northward Heat lost to atmosphere - moderates climate Carbon, oxygen moved to deep ocean for 10/100's years - sink Slowing of AMOC - more heat recirculates in gyre -> affects intensity of hurricanes -> affects rainfall in Sahel
How is AMOC transport measured?

Components

  • Florida Current
  • Ekman transport (wind-driven)
  • Geostrophic transport (density-driven)
  • Residual transport (zero net flow)

Geostrophic transport

  • Difference between E/W density drives N/S flow
  • Density measured close to continental slopes
Florida Current - most of Gulf Stream at this latitude, measured by undersea cables. Ekman - estimated from satellite wind data Geostrophic transport - difference in density between east and west drives flow northward or southward. Analagous to wind driven by differences between high and low pressure being along isobars. Residual - based on assumption that on timescales longer than a week or so, what goes into basin above 26°N must come out - zero net transport.

What happens if the AMOC slows down?

Air temperature

Northern Hemisphere freezes over in days!

Sea level

Rises 25 m!

Concern over AMOC slowdown was happening before 2013. Snapshot estimates based on 5 hydrographic sections showed a 30% slowdown between 1957 and 2004. The Day After Tomorrow - 2004 - 44% rating on Rotten Tomatoes website. Exaggerated as these effects are, they do have a grain of truth.
What happens if the AMOC slows down?

Air temperature

Climate model shows NH air temperatures cool by up to 8°C

(Jackson et al., 2009)

Sea level

Rose by 128 mm during a 30% downturn in AMOC during 2009/2010

(Goddard et al., 2015)

Climate model shows air temperature over UK & Ireland reducing by around 5°C. 30% downturn in AMOC in 2009/2010. Observed sea level along NE coast of USA. Coastal sea level north of New York City jumped by 128 mm. A 1-in-850 year event.
Concern about an AMOC slowdown led to a full-width observing array being deployed in 2004

Is this a long-term decline due to climate change or a cycle over 10 or so years? Short time series and high variability make it difficult to tell.

How can we improve detection of AMOC changes?
Reduce variability
Increase length of timeseries

Reduce variability by combining AMOC with a less noisy climate signal

e.g., cross-Atlantic density difference

(Baehr et al., 2007)

Using deep density differences reduces the detection time to around 30 years.

The climate signal must: 1) Not be noisy, i.e., have good signal-to-noise ration 2) Be dynamically linked to the AMOC - remember observed reduction in LNADW - that will lead to a change in density difference 3) Not be directly influenced by global warming, e.g. sea surface temperature Most of deep density differences that have a result are 1700 - 3700m - mostly UNADW (1100 to 3000m) with a little LNADW (3000 to 5000 m)
Can we reduce variability of AMOC?

Variability due to mesoscale (~100 km, 1 month) eddies is suppressed close to steep continental slopes, e.g., off the Bahamas

Mean AMOC transport and variability for different mooring configurations

AMOC noise is suppressed slightly by using measurements closer to the boundary

Variability of density with distance from boundary

Pinpointing where variability is lowest may allow us to look with more confidence at 'snapshot' data, i.e., increase the length of the timeseries.

Summary

  • The AMOC plays an important role in climate
  • A slowdown will impact climate in UK/Ireland and further afield
  • Detecting slowdown using the AMOC itself may need a timeseries of over 60 years
  • Using deep density, which has much lower variability, may reduce detection time by 50%
