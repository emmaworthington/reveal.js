What is the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation?
Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC)

(Srokosz and Bryden, Science, 2015)

  • Slowing of AMOC - more heat recirculates in gyre
  • Very large amounts of heat moved northward
  • Heat lost to atmosphere - moderates climate
    • affects intensity of hurricanes
    • affects rainfall in Sahel

What happens if the AMOC slows down?

Northern Hemisphere freezes over in days!

Sea level rises 25 m!

  • Public attention was raised over AMOC slowdown before 2013 - around 2004
  • The Day After Tomorrow - 2004 - 44% rating on Rotten Tomatoes website.
  • Exaggerated as these effects are, they do have a grain of truth.
What happens if the AMOC slows down?

Climate model shows NH air temperatures cool by up to 8°C

(Jackson et al., 2009)

Sea level rose by 128 mm during a 30% AMOC downturn

(Goddard et al., 2015)

  • Climate model shows air temperature over UK & Ireland reducing by around 5°C
  • 30% downturn in AMOC in 2009/2010.
  • Observed sea level along NE coast of USA.
  • Coastal sea level north of New York City increased by over 12 cm
  • A 1-in-850 year event
Concern about an AMOC slowdown led to a full-width observing array being deployed in 2004
RAPID Project

(from www.rapid.ac.uk)

Instruments on the moorings measure temperature, salinity, pressure and current velocity - twice a day

  • Moorings are nearly full height
  • Upper and lower NADW
  • Moorings close to edges of basin to estimate geostrophic flow

Data is collected by a research ship every 18 months

How can we improve detection of AMOC changes?
Reduce variability
Increase length of timeseries

Reduce variability by combining AMOC with a less noisy climate signal

Using deep cross-basin density difference reduces the detection time to 30 years.

(Baehr et al., 2007)

The climate signal must:
  1. Not be noisy, i.e., have good signal-to-noise ration
  2. Be dynamically linked to the AMOC - remember observed reduction in LNADW - that will lead to a change in density difference
  3. Not be directly influenced by global warming, e.g. sea surface temperature

Deep density differences that improve detection time are 1700 - 3700m - mostly UNADW (1100 to 3000m) with a little LNADW (3000 to 5000 m)

Can we reduce variability of AMOC?

Variability due to mesoscale (~100 km, 1 month) eddies is suppressed close to steep continental slopes, e.g., off the Bahamas

  • Mesoscale (radius ~100km, may persist for months) eddies move westward and increase variability at wb.
  • Eddies are suppressed as they reach the steep continental slope.
Mean AMOC transport and variability for different mooring configurations

AMOC noise is suppressed slightly by using measurements closer to the boundary

Variability of density with distance from boundary

Identifying where variability is lowest may allow us to say more about historical 'snapshot' data, i.e., increase the length of the timeseries.

If we can use density differences as a proxy for AMOC strength, low variability may mean we extend that 'snapshot' spatially and in time

Summary

  • A slowdown will impact climate in Ireland, UK and further afield
  • Detecting slowdown using the AMOC itself may need a timeseries 60+ years long
  • Using climate signals with low variability may reduce detection time by half
    • e.g., deep density measured close to steep continental slopes
