Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation

AMOC

RAPID mooring array at 26째N

(from www.rapid.ac.uk)

Instruments on the moorings measure temperature, salinity, pressure and current velocity

  • Upper layers of warm, salty water moving northwards to seas around Greenland
  • Water cools, becoming dense enough to sink
  • This and Antarctic Ocean are only regions of deep water formation
  • Cold, dense water returns equatorwards at depth
  • Very large amounts of heat moved northward
  • Heat lost to atmosphere - moderates climate
  • Slowing of AMOC - more heat recirculates in gyre
    • affects intensity of hurricanes
    • affects rainfall in Sahel due to ITCZ position
  • Moorings are nearly full height
  • Moorings close to edges of basin to estimate geostrophic flow
  • Measurements taken every 15 minutes but post-processing are 12-hourly
  • Schematic shows upper and lower NADW more clearly

Why do we want to simplify the dynamics?

Before 2004, measurements were taken by research ships crossing the Atlantic

RRS Discovery II leaving Woods Hole Oceanographic Institute in 1957

(Data from Bryden et al., (2005))

  • Only 5 hydrographic sections between 1957 and 2004.
  • Transport measured in Sverdrups, = volume per second
  • Appears to show AMOC slowdown.
  • How can we use other historical data to tell us about the state of the AMOC?

A simple linear regression

Atlantic Ocean potential temperature section at 24째N

World Ocean Circulation Experiment (1990 - 1997)

  • Potential temperature - temperature a water parcel would have if it were moved adiabatically to a reference pressure

Simple linear regression

Longworth et al. (2011)

Data from 39 CTD casts taken between 1981 and 2005

$$ \Delta T_{upper} = A\dot{}temp_{400} + B $$

Regression explains 53% of variance

Temperature chosen at western boundary at 400 dbar - mid-point of upper layer. Variability greater at western boundary and more measurements.

Simple linear regression using RAPID data

Thermocline (0 - 800m) transport predicted by western boundary temperature at 400 dbar

Only 9% of thermocline transport variance explained by regression

A slightly more complicated linear regression

Multiple linear regression

$$ \Delta T = A\dot{}temp_1 + B\dot{}sal_2 + C\dot{}temp_3 + D $$

Find the depth at which each variable gives the highest variance explained by the regression

Multiple linear regression using temperature and salinity anomalies

$$ \Delta T = A\dot{}temp_{780} + B\dot{}sal_{1020} + C\dot{}temp_{2840} + D $$

Now 39% of mid-ocean transport variance explained by regression

Multiple linear regression using density anomalies

$$ \Delta T = A\dot{}\rho_{740} + B\dot{}\rho_{940} + C\dot{}\rho_{2840} + D $$

67% of mid-ocean transport variance explained by regression

Summary

  • A three-layer regression captures nearly 70% of the variance of an AMOC component
  • Using temperature and salinity doesn't capture dynamics as well as density
  • Applying the regression to historical temperature and salinity data should extend our understanding of past AMOC states (within a level of uncertainty)
  • Allows us to use historical T/S data to give us a better picture of AMOC state
  • Unfortunate since historical salinity data (e.g. 1950s) is unreliable
